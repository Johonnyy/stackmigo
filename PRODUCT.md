# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Vibe-coder founders, engineers, and technical decision-makers. They build software (often AI-assisted / "vibe-coded") and ship fast. They are discerning: they can smell a generic template and a marketing team that never read the code. The landing site has to earn their respect on craft alone before it earns their trust on substance.

## Product Purpose

stackmigo is a B2B service that finds the security and liability problems in vibe-coded apps before users or attackers do. It exists because shipping-fast, AI-assisted development outruns the safety review that used to come with it, leaving founders exposed to breaches and legal liability they can't see. Success looks like a technical visitor reading the page and thinking "these people clearly know exactly where my app is going to break, and I want them to look at it."

(Background, not to be used as copy: the business previously sold AI automation; the current internal value-prop line about getting "sued or hacked" is directional only and must be rewritten, not reused.)

## Brand Personality

Sharp and authoritative. Three words: precise, credible, unflinching. Voice is the senior engineer who has seen the failure modes and names them plainly, without fear-mongering and without hype. Confident enough to be specific. Never condescending to an audience that writes code.

## Anti-references

- **Generic AI SaaS**: purple/indigo gradients, glassmorphism, floating blobs, the big-number hero-metric template, gradient text. The thing every AI startup ships.
- **Security clichés**: shield icons, padlocks, hooded hackers, fingerprint scanners, matrix-green code rain.
- **Cute / playful**: mascots, rounded bubbly illustrations, pastel consumer friendliness. Undercuts the seriousness of the stakes.

## Design Principles

1. **Practice what we preach.** A company that audits other people's craft must have impeccable craft itself. The site is the first proof of competence; it must never look vibe-coded or templated.
2. **Show, don't tell.** Earn authority with concrete failure modes, real findings, and specifics. Engineers trust evidence over adjectives.
3. **Respect the reader's intelligence.** The audience builds software. Be specific, never hand-wave, never condescend. Plain language about real risk.
4. **Confront the stakes calmly.** Name the danger (breach, liability) with the steadiness of someone who handles it routinely, not with alarm-bell clichés.
5. **Editorial clarity carries the weight.** Strong typographic hierarchy and structured statements do the work; decoration does not.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Best-effort, ship-fast. Reasonable defaults: legible contrast, keyboard navigability, semantic structure, and respect `prefers-reduced-motion`. Don't gate launch on a full WCAG audit; tighten toward AA after launch.
